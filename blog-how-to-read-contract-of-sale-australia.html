How to Read a Contract of Sale: What Every Australian Property Buyer Must Check

The contract of sale is the most legally binding document in the entire property purchase. Most buyers sign it without reading it. Here is what every page is telling you — and what can cost you dearly if you miss it.

RM
Rico M. — Principal Buyer Advocate
RICO Buyers Agent · 15+ Years Experience

Every year, Australian property buyers sign contracts of sale without fully understanding what they have agreed to. Some discover problems at settlement. Others discover them years later. A few discover them never — because the issue never surfaces. But the ones who get burned can lose their deposit, inherit undisclosed debts, take on structural defects they cannot prove were pre-existing, or find that the built-in wardrobes they fell in love with have already been removed by the vendor.

The contract of sale is the most important document in any property transaction. It defines exactly what you are buying, for how much, on what terms, and under what conditions the deal can fall over. Understanding how to read it — or ensuring the right professionals read it for you — is not optional. It is foundational due diligence.

Why the Contract Is the Most Important Document

Once you sign a contract of sale and the cooling-off period expires — or if you purchased at auction, where there is no cooling-off period — you are legally committed to completing the purchase. If you fail to settle, you can lose your deposit (typically 10% of the purchase price) and potentially face damages claims from the vendor on top of that. The contract governs everything: what you get, when you get it, what happens if the deal falls over, and what the vendor must and must not do before settlement.

Unlike the marketing material — which carries no legal weight — the contract is binding. What is in the contract is what you are buying. What is not in the contract, you have no legal claim to.

The Key Sections Every Buyer Must Understand

A standard Australian contract of sale typically runs between 20 and 60 pages, depending on the state and the complexity of the transaction. Here are the sections that demand your closest attention:

Section What It Contains What to Check
Parties Vendor and purchaser names Are the vendor's names on title? Any trustees or companies?
Property description Legal description, lot/plan number Does it match what you inspected? Any excluded land?
Purchase price Agreed sale price Correct amount? GST stated separately if applicable?
Deposit Amount and timing When is it due? Who holds it (vendor's solicitor or agent)?
Settlement date Completion date Achievable with your finance timeline? Early/late settlement provisions?
Inclusions/exclusions What stays, what goes Are all fixtures listed? Any chattels the vendor is removing?
Title documents Certificate of title, plan of subdivision Any easements, covenants, caveats, or encumbrances?
Vendor disclosures Known defects, notices, orders Any outstanding council orders, heritage overlays, flood zones?
Special conditions Buyer/vendor specific conditions Finance, inspection, strata clauses — see below

Special Conditions: Your Safety Net (or Your Trap)

Special conditions are additions to the standard contract terms. They can be proposed by either the buyer or the vendor, and they are the most negotiated part of any contract. A well-drafted special condition gives you an exit if something goes wrong. A poorly drafted one can leave you stuck — or worse, expose you to liability you did not know you had accepted.

Strong Special Conditions

  • Finance condition: clear approval amount, lender-specific, 14–21 day window
  • Building & pest inspection: buyer satisfied in their absolute discretion
  • Strata report: buyer satisfied with levies, minutes, and special levies
  • Long settlement: specific date agreed to allow you to sell existing property
  • Vacant possession: property delivered unoccupied by settlement

Dangerous Special Conditions

  • Finance condition: "best endeavours only" — not an unconditional exit
  • Inspection condition: "satisfactory to a reasonable buyer" — vague and disputed
  • Sunset clauses favoring the vendor on off-the-plan purchases
  • As-is conditions waiving implied warranties on property condition
  • Short finance periods (7 days) incompatible with actual approval timelines

What to Look for on the Title

The certificate of title (or title search) tells you who legally owns the property and what encumbrances are registered against it. Encumbrances are legal interests that other parties hold in the property — and they transfer with the land when you buy it. Common title issues include:

Easements: A right granted to another party to use part of your land. Common easements include drainage easements (allowing council or utilities to access underground pipes), rights of way (neighbours or utilities can cross your land), and sewerage easements. Easements can restrict what you can build on that part of the land and must be disclosed.

Covenants: Binding obligations that run with the land, often imposed by a developer at the time of subdivision. Covenants can restrict building materials, minimum house sizes, what type of structure can be built, or even aesthetic features. They can significantly affect what you can do with a property.

Caveats: A caveat is a formal notice that someone else has a legal interest in the property. A caveat lodged by a creditor, ex-spouse, or beneficiary can prevent settlement until it is removed. Any caveat on title must be investigated and resolved before you purchase.

"An easement for a stormwater drain that runs under your backyard might not affect how you use the property day-to-day — but it will absolutely prevent you from building a granny flat above it. Read the title documents before you get emotionally invested."

Fixtures vs Chattels: What Stays with the Property

One of the most common sources of buyer disappointment — and sometimes legal dispute — is the question of what is a fixture (stays with the property) and what is a chattel (the vendor's personal property that they can take with them). The general legal principle is that anything permanently attached to the land or building is a fixture. But "permanently attached" is open to interpretation.

Items that are almost always fixtures include fitted carpets, built-in wardrobes, dishwashers connected to plumbing, fixed lighting and ceiling fans, air conditioning units (fixed installations), and kitchen appliances connected to the building's services. Items that are generally chattels include freestanding fridges and washing machines, pot plants in pots, mirrors hanging on hooks, curtains and blinds (though sometimes disputed), and outdoor furniture.

The safest approach: if you expect something to be there on settlement day, get it listed in the contract's inclusions schedule. If the vendor wants to take something you consider a fixture, it should be listed in the exclusions schedule with an agreed price reduction or replacement.

GST Provisions

Goods and Services Tax applies to some property transactions in Australia. Residential property sold as a going concern is generally not subject to GST. However, GST can apply to commercial property, new residential property sold for the first time by a developer, and residential land sold without a dwelling. From July 2018, buyers of new residential property are required to withhold GST directly from the purchase price and remit it to the ATO at settlement — this does not increase your purchase price but is a critical settlement process step you must follow.

Never Sign Without Legal Review: No matter how straightforward a contract appears, never sign a contract of sale without having a qualified solicitor or licensed conveyancer review it first. This is not optional. A professional review typically takes 24–48 hours and costs $200–$500. The cost of missing a material issue in the contract can run into the tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars.

Vendor Disclosure Requirements by State

Each Australian state has different rules about what vendors must disclose to buyers. In Victoria, vendors must attach a Section 32 (Vendor's Statement) to the contract that discloses title details, encumbrances, outgoings, planning information, and building permits. In New South Wales, vendors must attach a contract with title documents and zoning certificates before they can legally market the property. In Queensland, vendors use a mandatory disclosure form and specific warnings. In Western Australia, the disclosure regime is less prescriptive.

Understanding what your state requires — and what the vendor is not obligated to disclose — shapes your due diligence approach. A buyers agent with state-specific experience knows what to look for beyond the mandatory disclosures.
