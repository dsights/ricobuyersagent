Mortgage Broker vs Bank: Which Is Better for Australian Homebuyers in 2026?

Your bank wants your business. A mortgage broker wants you to get the best deal — from any of 30+ lenders. Understanding that difference could save you thousands over your loan life.

PW
Piyush Wagji — Founder
RICO Buyers Agent · 3+ Years Experience

You've spent months searching for the right property. Now you need to finance it — and the question is whether to walk into your bank branch or call a mortgage broker. Most Australians default to their existing bank out of familiarity and inertia. But in 2026, with over 60 lenders actively competing for borrower business and mortgage brokers settling more than 74% of all new home loans nationally, there's a compelling argument that the bank-first approach is leaving money on the table.

This guide cuts through the noise and gives you a clear picture of what brokers do, how they get paid, what banks offer that brokers can't, and which path is genuinely better for most Australian homebuyers in the current environment.

The Fundamental Difference

A bank is a lender. When you walk into the Commonwealth Bank, ANZ, Westpac, or NAB, you are talking to someone whose job is to sell you their employer's products. They can offer you a range of their own home loan products — variable, fixed, offset, redraw — but they cannot recommend you go to a competitor, even if a competitor has a better product for your situation. Their legal obligation is to their employer, not to you.

A mortgage broker is an intermediary. Their job is to understand your financial situation, your borrowing objectives, and your preferences — then search their panel of lenders (typically 30–50 banks, non-bank lenders, and credit unions) to find the loan that best fits your needs. Under Australian credit law and the Best Interests Duty introduced in 2021, brokers are legally required to act in your best interests — not in the interests of any particular lender.

This structural difference is the most important thing to understand. A bank representative is a salesperson for one product range. A mortgage broker is an adviser with access to most of the market — and a legal obligation to recommend the best fit for you.

How Brokers Get Paid — and Why It Doesn't Cost You Extra

One of the most common misconceptions about mortgage brokers is that they charge fees that make them more expensive than going direct. In most cases, this is not true. The vast majority of Australian mortgage brokers are paid via commission from the lender — typically an upfront commission of around 0.5–0.65% of the loan amount at settlement, plus an ongoing trail commission of 0.1–0.2% per annum for as long as you hold the loan.

This commission is paid by the lender — not by you. From a borrower's perspective, the interest rate and fees on a loan arranged through a broker are typically identical to what you'd get applying directly to that same lender. The lender effectively pays the broker for the distribution service.

Some brokers do charge a fee-for-service on top of or instead of lender commission — this is more common for complex commercial or SMSF lending situations. For standard residential purchases, the service should be free to you. Always ask about the fee structure upfront, and ensure any broker you use discloses their commission arrangements in the Credit Proposal Disclosure document they're legally required to provide.

Broker vs Bank: An Honest Comparison

Mortgage Broker Advantages

  • Access to 30–50+ lenders and their full product range
  • Legally required to act in your best interest
  • Saves time — one application, multiple lender comparisons
  • Expertise in complex situations (self-employed, low deposit, etc.)
  • Access to specialist lenders not available to public
  • Ongoing advocacy — can refinance you if a better deal emerges
  • Free for most residential borrowers

Going Direct to Bank Advantages

  • May be faster if you're an existing, valued customer
  • Relationship banking — a known contact for issues
  • Some bank-exclusive products or loyalty discounts
  • Simpler process for straightforward, strong applications
  • Full control over the application process

For most Australian homebuyers — especially first home buyers, self-employed borrowers, those with less-than-perfect credit histories, or buyers with complex income structures — a mortgage broker will consistently outperform a single bank in terms of rate, terms, and lender flexibility. The bank-direct approach works best for highly qualified borrowers with strong income, significant assets, and a long relationship with their current institution who want a fast, simple transaction.

What Specialist Access Does a Broker Provide?

Beyond the major four banks, a good broker has access to a rich ecosystem of non-bank lenders, mutual banks, credit unions, and specialist lenders that most borrowers don't know exist. This is particularly valuable in several situations:

Non-residents and expat borrowers: Most of the major banks have significantly tightened their lending criteria for non-Australian residents and Australian expats earning foreign income. Specialist non-resident lenders on a broker's panel may still offer competitive terms for this cohort.

Self-employed borrowers: Banks are notoriously conservative with self-employed income assessment. Non-bank lenders and specialist lenders often use more flexible income assessment methods — alternative documentation loans — that enable self-employed buyers to access finance at rates comparable to traditional borrowers.

Low deposit borrowers: Lenders Mortgage Insurance (LMI) rates, approval criteria, and LVR policies vary significantly between lenders. A broker can identify which lenders offer the most favourable LMI terms for a given LVR and loan size, potentially saving thousands.

Construction loans: The complexity of progress payment lending for construction is handled very differently between lenders. An experienced broker who specialises in construction finance can find structures that protect the borrower through the build period.

What to Look for in a Good Mortgage Broker

Not all brokers are equal. The quality of your broker — their lender access, their assessment skills, their responsiveness — has a material impact on your experience and outcome. Here's what to look for:

Accreditation: Look for MFAA (Mortgage and Finance Association of Australia) or FBAA (Finance Brokers Association of Australia) membership. All brokers must hold an Australian Credit Licence (ACL) or operate under an ACL holder. You can verify credentials on ASIC's MoneySmart register.

Lender panel size: Ask how many lenders are on their panel and which major non-bank lenders are included. A panel of fewer than 20 lenders is limiting; 30+ is solid; 50+ gives comprehensive market coverage.

Specialist experience: If your situation is complex — self-employed, buying with foreign income, construction — ask about their specific experience in that area and ask for examples of comparable client outcomes.

Communication style: Your broker will be a critical partner through a stressful purchase process. Choose someone who explains things clearly, responds promptly, and proactively updates you. Poor broker communication is one of the leading causes of settlement delays.

Questions to Ask Your Broker

Question Why It Matters
How many lenders are on your panel? Indicates breadth of market access
Are you recommending this loan because it's best for me, or because it pays higher commission? Tests their transparency and Best Interests Duty compliance
What is the comparison rate — not just the advertised rate? Comparison rate includes fees and gives true cost comparison
What are the exit fees if I refinance within 3 years? Identifies any lock-in costs that affect flexibility
What does the offset account structure look like — and is there a fee? Offset accounts can save significant interest over time
How long does pre-approval typically take with this lender? Critical for auction buyers who need fast formal approval
What happens if my financial situation changes before settlement? Understanding your exposure to changed circumstances

The Role of the Buyers Agent Alongside the Broker

Experienced buyers agents and mortgage brokers work in complementary roles, and the best client outcomes typically come when both professionals are engaged early and communicate with each other throughout the process. The broker ensures the finance is structured correctly and approved in time for exchange; the buyers agent ensures the property is right, the price is fair, and the contract terms protect the buyer. When these two professionals are aligned, the buyer benefits from a seamless process.

The Bottom Line

For the overwhelming majority of Australian homebuyers, a mortgage broker will provide a better outcome than going direct to a single bank. The market access is broader, the service is free, the legal obligations favour you as a borrower, and the expertise — particularly for complex situations — is hard to replicate by walking into a branch. The 74% market share brokers now hold isn't an anomaly; it reflects the genuine value they provide.

That said, the quality of individual brokers varies enormously. A mediocre broker with a narrow lender panel who doesn't understand your situation can deliver a worse outcome than a proactive bank mortgage specialist who knows you well. Choose your broker carefully, ask the right questions, and treat the relationship as a partnership — because for the duration of your loan (potentially 30 years), it is.
