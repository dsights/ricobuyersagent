Interest Rates & Australian Property: What History Tells Us About Buying Near the Peak of a Rate Cycle

At 4.35%, Australian rates may be at or near their 2026 peak. Three of four big banks forecast no further hikes. Here's what has happened to property prices every single time rates peaked — and then fell — in the last 30 years.

The Pattern: What Happens to Property When Rates Fall

Australian property and interest rates have a well-documented relationship. When rates fall, borrowing capacity increases. When borrowing capacity increases, buyers can afford more. When buyers can afford more, competition increases and prices rise.

This relationship plays out predictably — and the investors who recognise the rate cycle peak and act before the crowd enjoy the largest gains.

Why Waiting for Rates to Fall Is the Wrong Strategy

Here's the fundamental problem with waiting: you are not the only buyer waiting. When the RBA cuts rates — as it has done following every rate cycle in modern Australian history — a surge of pent-up demand simultaneously enters the market.

That demand surge, hitting a market that has been constrained by the same affordability pressures you've been facing, creates a rapid compression in available stock and a rapid expansion in prices.

Buyers who waited for rates to fall in 2019–2020 watched in real-time as COVID-stimulus-driven rate cuts caused a 20–30% surge in prices in many markets within 18 months. The "relief" of lower rates was more than offset by higher prices.

The Rate Cut Paradox

You are waiting for rates to fall so you can afford to buy. But every other buyer in the country is doing the same thing. When rates fall, all of you enter the market simultaneously — competing for the same undersupplied housing stock. The result: prices rise faster than your repayments fall. The buyer who was already in the market before the cut wins on both sides.

The Strategy for 2026: Buy Now, Refinance Later

Sophisticated Australian buyers have a well-worn approach to high-rate environments: buy at current rates, lock in the purchase price, then refinance or simply benefit from lower variable rates when the cycle turns. The strategy has three components:

  • Buy strategically now: Target suburbs where investor retreat and rate-driven softness has created a buyer's market.
  • Choose variable or short-term fixed: Don't lock in a 5-year fixed rate at 6.5% — you'll miss the benefit of forecast cuts.
  • Refinance when rates fall: As rates decline, proactively refinance to capture the saving. On a $700K loan, each 0.5% drop saves $175/month.

The bottom line: In 2026, you cannot time the bottom of the interest rate cycle and the bottom of property prices simultaneously — because when rates fall, prices rise. The buyers who win are those who buy while sentiment is negative, then benefit from both lower rates and higher prices in the years that follow.
