Property Depreciation: How Australian Investors Maximise Tax Benefits in 2026

Depreciation is the most commonly unclaimed tax benefit available to Australian property investors. A single quantity surveyor report can unlock thousands in annual tax savings — and most investors either do not know it exists or have not got the report.

Every investment property in Australia wears out over time. The building deteriorates. The carpet gets worn. The hot water system ages. The oven element fails. In the eyes of the ATO, this gradual wearing out is a legitimate cost of earning rental income — and it is deductible. Property depreciation is a non-cash tax deduction that allows you to reduce your taxable income without spending a dollar in the year you claim it.

For a $700,000 investment property, annual depreciation deductions of $10,000–$15,000 are not uncommon for a well-constructed newer property. At a 37% marginal tax rate, that is a tax saving of $3,700–$5,550 per year. Over a 10-year holding period, that is $37,000–$55,500 in real cash savings — from a report that costs less than $700 to commission. The ROI on a depreciation schedule is extraordinary. Yet the majority of investment property owners do not have one.

What Property Depreciation Is

The ATO allows investment property owners to claim a deduction for the decline in value of their property over time. This deduction represents the economic reality that buildings age, structural elements degrade, and plant and equipment items (carpets, appliances, air conditioning) have finite useful lives. Unlike most deductions, depreciation does not require you to spend money in the year you claim it — it is a paper deduction based on the physical deterioration of the property.

This is what makes depreciation uniquely valuable: it directly improves your cash flow without requiring any additional outlay. Every dollar of depreciation you claim reduces your taxable income, reducing the tax you pay, and improving the net return on your investment.

The Two Types of Depreciation: Division 43 and Division 40

Feature Division 43 (Building Allowance) Division 40 (Plant & Equipment)
What it covers Structural elements: walls, roof, floors, concrete, wiring Removable items: carpets, appliances, blinds, hot water system, air conditioning
Depreciation rate 2.5% per year (straight line, 40-year life) Varies by item (5–40 years effective life)
Eligibility (construction date) Post-15 September 1987 (residential), post-20 July 1982 (commercial) All properties (with restrictions for second-hand — see below)
Available to second-hand buyers Yes, based on original construction cost Only for new items; second-hand Div 40 restricted since May 2017
Example deduction ($500k build cost) $500,000 × 2.5% = $12,500/year Varies; typically $3,000–$10,000/year for new builds

Which Properties Qualify

Division 43 — Building Allowance: Residential properties built after 15 September 1987 qualify for the building allowance. The deduction is calculated at 2.5% of the original construction cost (not the purchase price), spread over 40 years. A property purchased today that was built in 2010 for a construction cost of $400,000 would have 19 years of Div 43 claims remaining, worth $10,000 per year. Pre-1987 properties do not qualify for Div 43, though some commercial properties built after 1982 may qualify at a different rate.

Division 40 — Plant and Equipment: Prior to May 2017, investors buying existing (second-hand) residential properties could claim Div 40 depreciation on all existing plant and equipment items. This changed with the 2017 Budget: second-hand buyers can now only claim Div 40 on new items they install after purchase. Existing items (carpets, blinds, appliances) that were in the property at the time of purchase are no longer depreciable for second-hand buyers.

This change significantly reduced the depreciation benefits for buyers of existing properties. It is one of the reasons new builds have become increasingly attractive from a tax perspective — buyers of newly constructed properties retain full Div 40 entitlements.

"The 2017 plant and equipment changes fundamentally shifted the tax maths on new vs established investment properties. For investors in the 37–47% tax bracket, the depreciation advantage of a new build over an established property can add up to $3,000–$5,000 per year in extra tax savings."

Quantity Surveyor Reports: What They Cost and What You Get

To claim depreciation, you need a Tax Depreciation Schedule prepared by a qualified quantity surveyor (QS). The ATO requires this for property owners who did not construct the property themselves — you cannot estimate depreciation on your own. The cost of a tax depreciation schedule typically ranges from $600 to $700 for a standard residential investment property, though fees vary by property type, size, and location.

What you receive is a comprehensive report detailing every depreciable element of the property — from the building structure (Div 43) to individual plant and equipment items (Div 40). The report provides year-by-year projections of your deduction entitlements for the life of the property, and is accepted by the ATO as the basis for your depreciation claims. Your accountant uses this report when preparing your tax return.

2026 Budget Bonus for New Builds: The 2026 Federal Budget announced accelerated depreciation for newly constructed residential investment properties. Eligible new builds can write off a higher proportion of plant and equipment assets in the first three years, improving cash flow in the critical early years of ownership. Speak to your quantity surveyor and accountant about eligibility — this measure significantly improves the after-tax economics of buying new.

How Depreciation Improves Cash Flow

The cash flow impact of depreciation is direct and meaningful. Because depreciation is a non-cash deduction — you are not spending money when you claim it — every dollar of depreciation you claim reduces your taxable income without reducing your bank balance. The tax saving flows directly to your cash position.

For a negatively geared investment property (where rental income is less than total expenses), depreciation can significantly reduce or eliminate the out-of-pocket shortfall. Consider the following worked example for a $700,000 new townhouse purchased in Brisbane:

The depreciation deductions turn an investment that costs $5,897 per year out of pocket into one that costs less than $1,000 per year. The $14,300 difference in depreciation deductions generates an extra $5,291 in tax refund annually. Over five years, that is $26,455 in additional cash in your pocket — from a report that cost $650 to commission.

Should You Factor Depreciation into Your Buy Decision?

Absolutely — but it should be one input among many, not the primary driver. Properties should be purchased for their fundamental investment merits: location, land content, growth drivers, rental demand, and quality. A property with excellent depreciation but poor location fundamentals is still a poor investment.

That said, when comparing two properties with similar fundamental merits, the depreciation profile can be a significant differentiator. A new build with full Div 43 and Div 40 entitlements will generate meaningfully better after-tax cash flow than a 1970s brick home with no Div 43 and limited Div 40. For investors using negative gearing to build their portfolio, this cash flow difference determines how quickly they can service the loan and move on to the next property.

Depreciation and Its CGT Interaction

One important caveat on depreciation: Div 43 deductions claimed during the ownership period reduce the property's cost base for CGT purposes. This means that when you sell, your capital gain will be larger than if you had not claimed depreciation. However, because the deduction is taken at your full marginal rate now, and the resulting CGT is paid at half your marginal rate (after the 50% or 25% CGT discount), the maths almost always favour claiming depreciation aggressively. The tax is deferred, discounted, and paid later — which is the fundamental principle of good tax strategy.
