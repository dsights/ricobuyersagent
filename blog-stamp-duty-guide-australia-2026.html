Stamp Duty 2026: The Complete State-by-State Guide for Australian Property Buyers

Stamp duty can add tens of thousands of dollars to your property purchase — and most buyers don't realise how much until it's too late. Here's everything you need to know, state by state.

Piyush Wagji — Founder

You've saved your deposit. You've got pre-approval. You've found the property. Then your conveyancer sends through the settlement statement — and there it is: a six-figure stamp duty bill you hadn't fully budgeted for. It happens more often than you'd think. In New South Wales, buying at the median house price of around $1.1 million triggers a stamp duty bill of over $45,000. In Victoria, a similar purchase costs around $55,000 in transfer duty. These numbers are not small, and yet stamp duty remains one of the most misunderstood costs in Australian property.

This guide breaks down exactly what stamp duty is, how it works in every state and territory, what concessions are available, and — critically — how to plan around it strategically so it doesn't derail your purchase.

What Is Stamp Duty — and Why Does It Matter?

Stamp duty (known as transfer duty in Queensland and Western Australia) is a state and territory government tax applied to the transfer of property. It's calculated as a percentage of the purchase price — or the property's market value, whichever is higher — and must be paid as part of the settlement process. Unlike your mortgage, you can't spread stamp duty over 30 years. It's due at or before settlement, typically within 30 days of exchange in most states.

The tax gets its name from the old practice of physically stamping a document to certify payment — a relic of the 19th century that Australian governments have been reluctant to reform. Various inquiries, including the Henry Tax Review, have recommended abolishing stamp duty in favour of a broad-based land tax, and the ACT is the only jurisdiction actively phasing it out. For everyone else, stamp duty remains a significant and unavoidable upfront cost.

Stamp duty matters for three key reasons. First, it reduces your purchasing power — every dollar paid in stamp duty is a dollar not going toward your deposit or property. Second, it affects your loan-to-value ratio (LVR) — if you're borrowing at 90% LVR, a $35,000 stamp duty bill means you need $35,000 more in cash savings. Third, it creates a "price cliff" effect where buying just over a concession threshold can cost you dramatically more than buying just under it.

First Home Buyer Concessions and Exemptions

Every state offers some form of stamp duty relief for eligible first home buyers, but the rules vary significantly. The key variable is whether the concession applies to new builds only, established homes, or both — and what income or price limits apply.

In New South Wales, first home buyers purchasing a property for $800,000 or less pay zero stamp duty — a saving of up to $31,335. Those purchasing between $800,000 and $1,000,000 receive a partial concession on a sliding scale. NSW also offers an alternative First Home Buyer Choice scheme, which allows buyers to opt for an annual property tax instead of an upfront stamp duty payment — a useful option for buyers who expect to sell within a few years or who need to preserve cash for renovations.

Victoria's thresholds are lower: full exemption applies up to $600,000, with a concession between $600,000 and $750,000. Notably, Victoria applies a more generous concession to new homes and off-the-plan purchases, making the state particularly favourable for first home buyers targeting new construction.

Queensland offers the most generous new-build concession — eligible first home buyers can purchase a newly built home for up to $700,000 with no transfer duty. For established homes, the threshold is lower at $550,000. The differential is deliberate government policy to stimulate housing supply.

"In NSW, a first home buyer purchasing at $799,000 pays zero stamp duty — saving over $30,000. The same buyer purchasing at $801,000 pays $28,435. That $2,000 in purchase price costs them $28,000 in additional tax." — Piyush Wagji, Principal Buyer Advocate, RICO Buyers Agent

Example: Stamp Duty on a $750,000 Purchase

To make the numbers concrete, here's what a standard owner-occupier (non-first-home-buyer) pays in stamp duty on a $750,000 established property across key states:

$750,000 Property — Stamp Duty Comparison (Non-FHB, Owner-Occupier)

NSW: $29,240  |  VIC: $40,070  |  QLD: $27,090  |  WA: $22,865  |  SA: $32,330  |  NT: $23,928

For eligible first home buyers, NSW and QLD buyers may pay zero on this purchase. Victoria buyers would receive a partial concession. This represents savings of up to $40,000 simply by meeting the eligibility criteria.

These are not abstract numbers. A $40,000 difference in upfront costs can be the difference between buying now and waiting another 18 months to save — by which time the market may have moved significantly against you.

Foreign Buyer Surcharge — A Critical Warning

Important — Foreign Buyer Surcharge: Non-Australian citizens and permanent residents face an additional surcharge on top of standard stamp duty. In NSW, VIC, QLD and ACT this is 8% of the purchase price. On a $750,000 property, that's an additional $60,000 — bringing total transfer costs to over $89,000 in NSW. Temporary residents and foreign investors must factor this into their budgeting from day one. SA charges 7%, WA charges 7%, and NT does not apply a surcharge. Foreign Investment Review Board (FIRB) approval is also required in most cases, adding further costs and timeline considerations.

Stamp Duty vs Transfer Duty — What's the Difference?

Nothing, in practice. Queensland and Western Australia rebranded their stamp duty as "transfer duty" in the early 2000s — a name that more accurately reflects what the tax represents: the duty payable upon transferring the title of a property from one owner to another. The calculation methodology and the obligation to pay are identical. If your Queensland conveyancer mentions transfer duty, they are talking about stamp duty.

Some people confuse transfer duty with "transfer fees" charged by state land registries to process a title transfer. These are separate, much smaller amounts (typically $100–$500) and should not be conflated with stamp duty.

Strategic Tips to Minimise Your Stamp Duty Burden

While you can't avoid stamp duty entirely (unless you qualify for an exemption), there are legitimate ways to reduce your exposure or plan around it strategically.

1. Know your threshold — and respect it. If you're a first home buyer in NSW, buying at $799,000 instead of $810,000 saves you approximately $28,000 in stamp duty. This is not a trivial decision, and a skilled buyers agent can often negotiate a purchase price that keeps you under a meaningful threshold.

2. Consider off-the-plan for new-build concessions. Several states offer significantly reduced or zero stamp duty on off-the-plan purchases for both first home buyers and investors. The trade-off is construction risk and a delayed settlement, but the financial benefit can be substantial.

3. Check eligibility carefully. First home buyer exemptions typically require you to: be an Australian citizen or permanent resident, not have previously owned residential property in Australia, intend to occupy the property as your principal place of residence for at least 12 continuous months. Foreign ownership interests, even partial ones from a previous relationship, can disqualify you.

4. Use a buyers agent to negotiate below key thresholds. At RICO, one of the first things we assess is whether a property is priced near a stamp duty threshold — and whether there is room to negotiate the purchase price down to capture a concession. We've helped multiple clients save their entire stamp duty bill through strategic negotiation alone.

5. Factor stamp duty into your borrowing strategy. Some lenders will allow stamp duty to be included in the loan amount under certain conditions. Discuss this with your mortgage broker early — it can materially change how much deposit you need in hand at settlement.

The ACT Model — The Future of Property Tax?

The Australian Capital Territory is in the midst of a 20-year transition away from stamp duty entirely, replacing it with a broad-based annual land tax. For buyers, this means lower upfront costs but ongoing annual charges. Proponents argue this is more economically efficient and doesn't discourage mobility — people are no longer penalised for downsizing or upsizing when their family circumstances change. Critics point out that annual land taxes are harder to budget for and represent a permanent, ongoing cost rather than a one-time payment.

Whether other states follow the ACT's lead remains to be seen. NSW's First Home Buyer Choice scheme is a partial step in this direction. But for now, all other states remain committed to upfront stamp duty as a major revenue source — collectively raising over $20 billion per year nationally.

The Bottom Line

Stamp duty is one of the largest upfront costs in any property purchase, and the variation between states — and between eligible and non-eligible buyers — is enormous. A first home buyer in NSW purchasing at $799,000 pays zero. An investor in Victoria purchasing the same price point pays over $40,000. A foreign buyer in either state pays tens of thousands more on top.

The key is to understand your position before you start shopping — not after you've fallen in love with a property. Know which state you're buying in, whether you qualify for any concessions, what your threshold is, and how stamp duty fits into your overall cash position.
