Capital Gains Tax for Australian Property Investors: The Complete 2026 Guide

The 2026 Federal Budget just changed the CGT discount rules for new property purchases. Here is everything you need to know to protect your investment returns and plan your next move.

PW
Piyush Wagji — Founder

Capital Gains Tax — three words that can cost Australian property investors tens of thousands of dollars if they are not properly understood. CGT is the tax you pay on the profit made when you sell an investment property, and it is one of the most significant — and most misunderstood — costs in the property investment lifecycle. With the 2026 Federal Budget introducing changes to the CGT discount rate for new purchases, understanding exactly how CGT works has never been more critical.

Whether you are buying your first investment property or managing a portfolio of five, this guide gives you a complete, practical breakdown of how CGT applies to Australian property in 2026 — including how to legally minimise what you owe.

What Is Capital Gains Tax on Property?

Capital Gains Tax is not a separate tax in Australia — it is part of your income tax. When you sell an asset (including an investment property) for more than you paid for it, the profit (the "capital gain") is added to your taxable income for that financial year, and you are taxed at your marginal rate.

CGT was introduced in Australia on 20 September 1985. Any property purchased before that date is not subject to CGT. Any property purchased on or after that date — and subsequently sold at a profit — triggers a CGT event.

Crucially, your principal place of residence (PPOR) — the home you live in — is generally fully exempt from CGT. This is one of the most powerful tax advantages available to Australian homeowners, and we will cover the rules and partial exemptions in detail below.

How CGT Is Calculated: The Basics

The fundamental CGT calculation is straightforward: Capital Gain = Sale Price minus Cost Base. That gain is then added to your income and taxed accordingly. But the devil, as always, is in the detail.

Your cost base is not simply the price you paid for the property. The ATO allows you to include a range of additional costs that increase your cost base — effectively reducing your taxable gain. These include:

  • Purchase price of the property
  • Stamp duty paid at purchase
  • Legal and conveyancing fees (purchase and sale)
  • Real estate agent commission on sale
  • Capital improvements made to the property (new kitchen, bathroom renovation, extension)
  • Costs of defending or establishing your title
  • Certain borrowing costs not previously claimed as a tax deduction

Important: Repairs vs Capital Improvements

Ongoing maintenance and repairs (painting, fixing a leaking tap) are deductible in the year they occur and do NOT increase your cost base. Capital improvements that add value or extend the property's useful life (a new deck, structural changes) are added to the cost base. Getting this distinction right is essential — your accountant can help.

The 50% CGT Discount Explained

One of the most generous concessions in the Australian tax system — and a cornerstone of property investment strategy — is the 50% CGT discount. If you hold a property for more than 12 months before selling, you only pay tax on half your capital gain. The other half is simply disregarded by the ATO.

For an investor on the top marginal tax rate of 47% (including Medicare levy), this effectively means paying tax at 23.5% on the gain rather than 47%. For an investor on a 37% marginal rate, the effective CGT rate drops to just 18.5%. This is enormously powerful and explains why the "buy and hold" strategy has long been favoured by sophisticated Australian investors.

"The 12-month holding rule is one of the most valuable free strategies available to any property investor. Selling just one week before the 12-month mark could cost you tens of thousands of dollars in unnecessary tax."

2026 Budget Changes: What Has Changed for New Purchases

2026 Budget Alert: The Federal Government announced in the May 2026 Budget that the CGT discount for individuals will be reduced from 50% to 25% for properties purchased after the Budget announcement date. Existing properties purchased before the announcement are grandfathered and retain the 50% discount. This change significantly affects the after-tax returns on new investment property purchases.

This is the most significant change to property CGT rules in decades. Under the new framework, an investor who purchases an investment property in June 2026 and sells it five years later will only have 25% of their capital gain disregarded — not 50%. The practical impact on returns is substantial.

The government's stated rationale is to improve housing affordability by reducing the tax incentive for speculative property investment. Whether that objective will be achieved is a matter of ongoing debate. What is not debatable is the mathematical impact on your returns.

The difference between the pre-budget and post-budget scenarios is $47,000 in additional tax on a $400,000 gain. For investors weighing up whether to accelerate a purchase before the rule change fully beds in, or whether to restructure ownership (for instance through a superannuation fund, which has its own CGT rules at 10% after 12 months), this change demands careful consideration.

The PPOR Exemption: Your Home Is Generally Tax Free

The Principal Place of Residence (PPOR) exemption is the single most valuable CGT concession in Australian tax law. If you sell the home you have been living in, the capital gain is entirely exempt from CGT — regardless of how large that gain is, and regardless of how long you held it (as long as it was your main residence throughout that period).

To qualify for the full PPOR exemption, the property must have been your main residence for the entire period of ownership, must not have been used to produce income during that time, and must sit on land of 2 hectares or less. The exemption covers the house itself plus up to 2 hectares of adjacent land.

Partial Exemptions: When You Rent Out Your Home

Life is rarely straightforward, and tax law reflects that. Several situations result in a partial — rather than full — CGT exemption on a property that was once your main residence:

The Six-Year Rule: If you move out of your PPOR and rent it out, you can continue to treat it as your main residence for CGT purposes for up to six years. This is an extraordinarily powerful concession that many Australians are unaware of. If you sell within that six-year window, you may owe no CGT at all, even though the property was rented during that period. The catch: you cannot simultaneously claim another property as your PPOR during this time.

Mixed-use properties: If you used part of your home for income-producing purposes — running a business from home, renting out a room — a proportional partial exemption applies. The CGT calculation apportions the gain based on the floor area used for income production and the proportion of time it was used that way.

Properties that were never a PPOR: If you buy an investment property that you never live in, there is no PPOR exemption, and the full CGT rules apply.

CGT Timing Strategies for Investors

Because CGT is added to your ordinary income in the year of sale, when you sell matters enormously. Smart investors use several strategies to reduce their CGT liability legally:

Sell in a low-income year: If you are planning to retire, take parental leave, or take a career break, your taxable income in that year will be lower — meaning the capital gain pushes you into a lower tax bracket. Timing a sale to coincide with a low-income year can save tens of thousands of dollars.

Always hold past 12 months: This seems obvious, but the temptation to sell quickly in a rising market can override long-term thinking. The CGT discount alone is worth waiting for.

Offset gains with losses: Capital losses from other assets (shares, other properties sold at a loss) can be offset against capital gains from property. This is called "loss harvesting" and can meaningfully reduce your CGT bill in the year of sale.

Instalment sales: In some circumstances, structuring a sale over two financial years (through extended settlement or vendor finance arrangements) can split the gain across two income years, potentially keeping you in a lower tax bracket in each year.

The Role of Depreciation Schedules in CGT

Depreciation deductions claimed during the ownership period reduce your cost base, which increases your ultimate capital gain. This is a nuance many investors miss. Every dollar of depreciation deducted reduces your cost base by that same dollar — so when you sell, you will have a larger taxable gain than you might expect.

This does not mean you should avoid claiming depreciation — the tax saving in the year of the deduction is immediate and certain, while the increased CGT at sale is deferred and discounted. But it does mean that when planning a sale, you need to account for the reduced cost base in your CGT calculation.

Notice that the effective tax rate on the $99,500 actual gain is only $18,408 — an effective rate of 18.5%, despite being on a 37% marginal rate. This is the power of the CGT discount in action. Without the discount, tax would be $36,815. The discount saves this investor $18,407.
