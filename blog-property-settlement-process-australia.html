Property Settlement in Australia: Step-by-Step Guide from Exchange to Keys

Settlement day should be exciting — not a source of anxiety. Understanding each step of the process, from exchanging contracts to receiving your keys, means nothing catches you off guard.

RM
Rico M. — Principal Buyer Advocate

The moment you exchange contracts on a property is one of the most significant of your life. From that point forward, a series of legal, financial, and practical steps unfold that culminate in settlement day — the day the property legally becomes yours. For first-time buyers especially, this process can feel opaque and stressful, largely because most people only go through it once or twice in their lifetime.

This guide demystifies the entire journey. We'll walk through each stage from exchange to handover, explain what's happening behind the scenes, and highlight the common mistakes that cause delays, additional costs, or — in worst cases — settlement falling through entirely.

What Is Settlement — and Why Does It Matter?

In Australian property law, settlement is the legal completion of a property transaction. It's the moment when the title of the property is transferred from the seller's name to the buyer's name, the purchase price is paid in full (including via your mortgage drawdown), and possession of the property passes to the buyer.

Settlement is distinct from exchange. Exchange happens when both parties sign identical copies of the contract and swap them — creating a binding agreement. Settlement is when that agreement is fulfilled. The period between exchange and settlement — the settlement period — is when all the preparatory work happens: your lender finalises your loan, your conveyancer conducts final searches, stamp duty is calculated and paid, and everything is made ready for the transfer.

Getting settlement right matters because the consequences of errors or delays are significant. A delayed settlement can breach the contract, triggering penalty interest at rates typically 10–12% per annum on the outstanding balance. A failed settlement — where settlement doesn't proceed on the agreed date through a party's fault — can result in contract termination, forfeiture of the deposit, and legal action.

The Six Key Steps: Exchange to Keys

While every transaction has its nuances, the settlement process follows a broadly consistent structure across Australian states. Here are the six stages you'll move through.

Step 1 — Exchange of Contracts. Exchange occurs when both parties have signed the contract and the buyer has paid the initial deposit (typically 10% of the purchase price, though 5% is sometimes negotiated). In most states this creates a binding contract immediately. In NSW, Victoria, and QLD there is typically a cooling-off period post-exchange (usually 2–5 business days depending on the state) during which the buyer can rescind — though a 0.25% penalty of the purchase price applies. Property purchased at auction does not attract a cooling-off period in any state.

Step 2 — Cooling Off Period. If applicable, the cooling-off period gives buyers a limited window to withdraw from the contract. This is the time to have your building and pest inspection completed if you haven't already, review the contract with your solicitor, and confirm your finance position. Most buyers who have done proper due diligence before exchange will simply let the cooling-off period expire without issue.

Step 3 — Pre-Settlement Period. This is the busy middle phase where your conveyancer conducts title searches, reviews any encumbrances or easements, calculates adjustments (rates, water, council charges) for the settlement statement, and coordinates with your lender. Your lender will conduct their own valuation and finalise your loan approval. You should also be arranging building insurance — in most states the risk passes to the buyer at exchange, so you're responsible for the property from that point. Confirm with your conveyancer when risk passes in your state.

Step 4 — Pre-Settlement Inspection. You are entitled to conduct a pre-settlement inspection of the property, typically within the last few days before settlement. This is not a building inspection — it's a condition check to confirm the property is in the same condition as when contracts were exchanged, that no inclusions have been removed, and that the property has been left reasonably clean. If you discover issues — a fixture has been removed, damage has occurred — raise them immediately with your conveyancer. Do not wait until after settlement.

Step 5 — Settlement Day. In Australia, most settlements now occur electronically via PEXA (Property Exchange Australia), the national e-conveyancing platform used in NSW, VIC, QLD, WA, SA, ACT, NT, and TAS. On settlement day, your conveyancer and the seller's conveyancer both log in to the PEXA workspace. Your lender electronically transfers the loan funds. The purchase price (less your deposit already held by the vendor's agent) is disbursed to the seller. The title is transferred and registered. The whole process typically takes minutes — not hours — when everything is in order. Once settlement is confirmed, your agent will release the keys to you.

Step 6 — Post-Settlement. After settlement, your conveyancer will notify you, the key collection will be arranged, and you'll receive confirmation that the title has been registered in your name. Follow up to ensure your rates notices and utilities are transferred, notify your insurer, and if it's an investment property, get your property manager in place immediately. Keep all settlement documents safely — you'll need them for tax purposes and when you eventually sell.

"Settlement day itself is actually the least stressful part of the process — if you've done everything right in the six weeks beforehand. The stress comes from being unprepared, not from the day itself." — Rico M., Principal Buyer Advocate, RICO Buyers Agent

Settlement Periods: 30, 42, 60, 90 Days — Which Is Right for You?

The settlement period is negotiated between buyer and seller at exchange. The most common period is 42 days (six weeks) for residential property, but 30 days and 60 days are also frequently used. Longer settlements of 90 days or more are rarer but do occur, particularly for off-the-plan purchases or where a vendor needs time to find alternative accommodation.

Settlement Period Best For Consideration
30 Days Cash buyers, unconditional finance pre-approval, seller wants fast exit Less time for finance, searches, insurance — high pressure
42 Days Standard purchase, most residential transactions Comfortable for most buyers and sellers — the default in most markets
60 Days Buyers who need more time to sell their current property or arrange finance Seller may require a higher deposit or premium for longer wait
90+ Days Off-the-plan, vendor needs extended possession, complex transactions Greater market risk — property values can change significantly

A shorter settlement period can be a competitive advantage in a hot market — sellers often prefer buyers who can move quickly. A buyers agent can negotiate settlement period as part of the overall offer, and in some cases, flexibility on settlement has helped our clients secure properties ahead of competing offers at a higher price.

Conveyancer vs Solicitor — Who Should You Use?

Both licensed conveyancers and solicitors can handle property settlement in most Australian states. The difference lies in scope: a conveyancer is a specialist in property transfers and is often more cost-effective for straightforward residential purchases. A solicitor (property lawyer) can handle conveyancing but also has the broader legal expertise to advise on more complex matters — disputes, unusual contract terms, title issues, or FIRB requirements for foreign buyers.

For most standard residential purchases, a reputable licensed conveyancer is excellent value. Fees typically range from $1,500 to $3,500 depending on the state, complexity, and whether disbursements (searches, PEXA fees, etc.) are included. Always get a quote that clearly separates professional fees from disbursements — some firms advertise low base fees but charge significantly for searches and disbursements.

If your purchase involves any complexity — a deceased estate, a property with unusual encumbrances, a foreign buyer with FIRB requirements, or a dispute about contract terms — use a property solicitor.

Common Settlement Delays and How to Avoid Them

Common Causes of Settlement Delays:
  • Finance not finalised: Your lender hasn't received all required documents. Submit everything promptly and follow up relentlessly.
  • Outstanding searches: Council or title searches taking longer than expected. Your conveyancer should allow adequate lead time.
  • Seller not ready to vacate: Seller delays moving out. Ensure the contract includes a vacant possession clause with a defined time.
  • Discrepancy in settlement figures: Rates adjustment figures don't match between parties. Usually resolved quickly but can add a day or two.
  • PEXA technical issues: Rare but possible. Your conveyancer should have contingency protocols.
  • Title issues: Encumbrances, caveats, or mortgages that haven't been discharged. A thorough title search should identify these early.

The single most important thing you can do to avoid a delayed settlement is to provide your lender and conveyancer with every document they request as quickly as possible. Delays in the buyer's own documentation are the leading cause of settlement extensions. Keep a checklist, respond to emails same-day during the settlement period, and don't book your removalists until settlement is confirmed.

What to Do on Settlement Day

For most buyers in the PEXA era, settlement day itself requires very little active participation. Your conveyancer handles the electronic lodgement. That said, here's a practical checklist for the day:

  • Confirm with your conveyancer that everything is in order the day before
  • Complete your pre-settlement inspection (ideally the day before or morning of)
  • Have your ID ready in case it's needed
  • Know when and where to collect keys — typically from the selling agent's office
  • Arrange your removalists or access for after settlement is confirmed (not before)
  • Ensure your building insurance is active from the settlement date
  • Celebrate — you own a property

The Bottom Line

Property settlement doesn't have to be stressful. Armed with the right professional support — a good conveyancer, an engaged lender, and ideally a buyers agent who has been through the process hundreds of times — the period from exchange to keys should be smooth, predictable, and ultimately joyful. The key is preparation: get your finance in order before exchange, choose a competent conveyancer, communicate promptly, and know your rights at every stage. The rest is process.
