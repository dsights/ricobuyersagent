Building & Pest Inspection: What Every Australian Buyer Must Know Before They Sign

A $600 inspection can save you $60,000 in hidden defects, termite damage, and structural repairs. Here's how to do it properly — and use the results to your advantage.

RM
Rico M. — Principal Buyer Advocate
RICO Buyers Agent · 15+ Years Experience

Every year, Australian property buyers sign contracts and hand over hundreds of thousands of dollars for properties concealing serious structural problems, active termite infestations, asbestos, rising damp, failing roofs, and electrical hazards. Many of them had no idea — because they skipped, or rushed, the building and pest inspection. Some chose not to pay the $500 inspection fee in a competitive market. Others were told by a selling agent that "the property is in great condition" — a statement that carries zero legal weight and zero financial protection.

A professional building and pest inspection is the most important $400–$800 you will spend in any property purchase. This guide explains exactly what it covers, how to read and interpret the report, which findings should concern you, and — crucially — how to use an inspection report as a negotiation tool to reduce your purchase price or compel vendor repairs.

Why Building and Pest Inspections Are Non-Negotiable

Unlike in some overseas markets, Australian property is sold largely on a "buyer beware" (caveat emptor) basis. Vendors are required to disclose certain matters — typically those prescribed by state legislation — but the disclosure obligations vary by state and do not require vendors to proactively identify or disclose every defect. In Queensland, for example, vendors must complete a seller disclosure statement covering a specific list of items, but undisclosed defects the vendor was unaware of are generally not grounds for contract rescission.

This means that once you sign an unconditional contract and settlement occurs, you own the property — defects and all. Your legal remedies for undisclosed defects are limited and expensive. The building and pest inspection is your primary protection mechanism, and it must happen before you commit unconditionally to the purchase.

In most states, buyers can include a "subject to building and pest inspection" condition in the contract, giving them the right to withdraw or renegotiate if the inspection reveals unsatisfactory findings. At auction, this protection is generally not available — which is why buyers agent-led pre-auction inspections are so important. Never bid at auction on a property you haven't inspected.

What Do Building and Pest Inspectors Actually Check?

A combined building and pest inspection is typically conducted by two inspectors simultaneously — a licensed building inspector and a licensed pest inspector — or by a single qualified professional holding both licences. The inspection covers all accessible areas of the property and takes 1–3 hours depending on the size and complexity of the building.

Building inspection covers: structural integrity of foundations, walls, and roof frame; condition of the roof covering (tiles, iron, etc.); gutters and drainage; condition of external cladding; windows and doors (operation, seals, frames); internal walls, ceilings, and floors; wet areas (bathrooms, laundry, kitchen) for moisture and waterproofing integrity; electrical switchboard (visible condition only — not a full electrical inspection); hot water system condition; visible plumbing; carport, garage, shed, and other outbuildings; retaining walls and site drainage.

Pest inspection covers: evidence of active termite activity or previous termite damage; conditions conducive to termite activity (moisture, timber-to-soil contact, etc.); evidence of other timber pests including borers and wood decay fungi; accessible subfloor, roof void, and external perimeter inspection.

Note what inspections do not cover: behind walls and under floors (unless access is available), swimming pool and spa safety compliance (requires a separate pool inspector), asbestos testing (a visual identification may be made, but testing requires a licensed asbestos assessor), smoke alarm compliance, or detailed electrical/plumbing/gas compliance (separate trades inspections are recommended for older properties).

How to Read an Inspection Report

Australian building inspection reports are prepared in accordance with Australian Standard AS 4349.1 (building inspections) and AS 3660.2 (pest inspections). Most reports classify findings into two categories:

Classification Definition Buyer Action
Major Defect A defect of significant magnitude where rectification must be carried out urgently to prevent unsafe conditions, or failure of the building or element. Includes active termites, structural cracking, failing roof structure, major moisture damage. Negotiate price reduction, request vendor remediation, or consider walking away. Get repair quotes.
Minor Defect A defect of minor significance which does not require immediate rectification but should be monitored. Includes minor cracking, worn seals, sticking doors, minor roof tile displacement. Factor into your maintenance budget. May support a modest price reduction. Generally not grounds for rescission.
Safety Hazard Conditions presenting an immediate risk to life or property — broken steps, exposed electrical wiring, loose handrails, failing balustrades. Must be addressed. If vendor won't remediate, factor rectification cost into your offer. Never ignore.
Further Investigation Items that require specialist assessment beyond the scope of the inspection — suspected asbestos, hidden moisture source, unusual cracking patterns. Engage appropriate specialist before proceeding unconditionally. Don't guess.

A key principle: every older Australian property will have some minor defects. A well-maintained 30-year-old home will still generate a report with a list of minor items. Don't panic at the length of the list — focus on the severity classification and the specific nature of any major defects.

"A building report is not a pass-fail document. It's a risk register. Your job is to understand the magnitude of each risk, what it would cost to remediate, and whether the current purchase price adequately reflects those risks." — Rico M., Principal Buyer Advocate, RICO Buyers Agent

10 Red Flags That Should Give You Pause

Red Flags — Take These Seriously:
  1. Active termite activity: Not just old damage — live termites present. Remediation is urgent and expensive.
  2. Structural cracking: Diagonal or stair-step cracking in brick or masonry — especially around corners of doors and windows — can indicate foundation movement.
  3. Subfloor moisture: Wet or damp subfloor creates ideal termite and mould conditions. Identify the source before proceeding.
  4. Failing roof frame: Cracked, broken, or undersized rafters or purlins. Roof replacement or major repair is a six-figure cost on many properties.
  5. Asbestos suspected: Pre-1990 properties commonly contain asbestos in fibro cladding, eaves, ceiling tiles, and floor vinyl. Testing required.
  6. Rising damp: Salt deposits or tide marks on lower internal walls. Can be costly to remediate — sometimes involving full wall reconstruction.
  7. Failing retaining walls: Leaning, bulging, or cracked retaining walls carry collapse risk and significant remediation cost.
  8. Non-compliant additions: Structures added without council approval — sheds, carports, additional rooms. May need to be removed or retrospectively approved at cost.
  9. Electrical knob-and-tube wiring: Pre-1960s properties may have old wiring that insurance companies refuse to cover without replacement.
  10. Ongoing drainage issues: Evidence of pooling water, soil saturation, or drainage failures near the foundation. Water management problems rarely self-correct.

Using the Report to Negotiate

A building and pest report is not just a risk document — in the hands of a skilled negotiator, it's a powerful tool to reduce the purchase price or compel vendor remediation before settlement.

The first step is to get remediation quotes from licensed tradespeople — builders, pest control operators, electricians — for any major defects identified. This gives you a dollar figure to work with. Then present the quotes to the selling agent, framing the conversation around what it will cost you to bring the property to the condition you reasonably expected when you made your offer. A well-reasoned request for a price reduction of $15,000 backed by three builder's quotes is far more compelling than a vague "the report showed some problems."

Vendors have options: they can reduce the price, agree to remedy the defects before settlement (with you retaining the right to inspect post-remediation), or hold firm — in which case you must decide whether the property at the current price still represents fair value given the known defects. Having a buyers agent manage this negotiation is valuable because experienced advocates know what repair costs are realistic, what vendors are likely to concede, and how to frame the conversation professionally.

Queensland vs Other States: Disclosure Rules

Queensland is the only Australian state with a formal statutory seller disclosure regime (the Property Law Act 2023, effective 2025) requiring vendors to provide a prescribed disclosure statement covering specific items including flood history, encumbrances, and known defects. However, even in Queensland, disclosure obligations do not replace the need for an independent inspection — the disclosure framework covers legally prescribed matters, not building condition.

In NSW, Victoria, WA, and SA, vendor disclosure obligations are more limited and are primarily focused on title and legal matters rather than physical condition. Western Australia has recently introduced improved consumer protections for buyers, but building condition remains primarily a buyer-beware matter. In all states, your building and pest inspection is the primary mechanism for identifying physical defects.

Combined vs Separate Inspections

Many buyers opt for a combined building and pest inspection — one company, one appointment, one report. This is cost-effective ($500–$800 for combined, vs $350–$500 each separately) and convenient. However, be aware that some combined inspection companies use a single inspector holding both licences, which can mean less specialised expertise in each area.

For properties with a higher pest risk — older Queensland homes, coastal properties with subfloor access, any property in a known termite belt — consider separate specialist inspectors. A building inspector who focuses entirely on structural matters and a specialist pest inspector who uses thermal imaging and moisture metres may identify issues that a generalist combined inspector misses.

The Bottom Line

A building and pest inspection is one of the highest-return expenditures in any property transaction. At $400–$800, it's a rounding error compared to the purchase price — but the information it provides is invaluable. It protects you from hidden defects, gives you grounds to negotiate the purchase price, and provides the data you need to make an informed decision about whether to proceed.

Never skip it. Never rush it. Never use an inspector recommended exclusively by the selling agent. Choose an independent, fully licensed inspector with professional indemnity insurance. Read the report carefully. Ask questions if you don't understand something. And if you find something significant — use it. A well-managed inspection report, in the right hands, is one of the most powerful negotiating tools available to property buyers.
