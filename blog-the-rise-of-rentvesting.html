The Rise of Rentvesting: Is It the Right Strategy for You?

For many young Australians, the dream of owning a home in their desired location feels increasingly out of reach. But what if there was a way to get onto the property ladder without sacrificing your lifestyle?

Enter rentvesting. This popular property investment strategy involves renting where you want to live and buying an investment property where you can afford. It's a way to build wealth and secure your financial future, while still enjoying the lifestyle you love.

But is rentvesting the right strategy for you? Let's explore the pros and cons.

The Pros of Rentvesting

  • Lifestyle Flexibility: Renting gives you the freedom to live where you want, whether it's close to work, the beach, or your favourite cafes. You can also upsize, downsize, or relocate without the hassle of buying and selling.
  • Earlier Market Entry: By buying in a more affordable area, you can get onto the property ladder sooner with a smaller deposit.
  • Wealth Building: Your investment property can grow in value over time, building your wealth and equity.
  • Tax Benefits: As a property investor, you may be able to claim a range of tax deductions, such as interest on your loan, property management fees, and maintenance costs.

The Cons of Rentvesting

  • No First Home Owner Grants: In most cases, you won't be eligible for the First Home Owner Grant or other government concessions for first-home buyers.
  • Ongoing Rental Costs: You'll still have to pay rent, which can be a significant ongoing expense.
  • Capital Gains Tax: When you sell your investment property, you'll have to pay capital gains tax on any profit you make.
  • Less Security: As a renter, you have less security than a homeowner. Your landlord could decide to sell the property or increase the rent.

Is Rentvesting Right for You?

Rentvesting can be a smart strategy for some, but it's not for everyone. Ask yourself the following questions:

  • Are you happy to rent for the long term?
  • Are you comfortable with the responsibilities of being a landlord?
  • Have you spoken to a financial advisor about the potential risks and rewards?
  • Are you willing to invest in a location that you may not want to live in?
