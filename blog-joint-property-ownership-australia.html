Joint Property Ownership in Australia: Tenants in Common vs Joint Tenants Explained

The way you hold title to a property has profound implications for your estate, your taxes, and what happens if the relationship breaks down. Most buyers choose without understanding the difference.

RM
Rico M. — Principal Buyer Advocate
RICO Buyers Agent · 15+ Years Experience

When two or more people buy a property together in Australia, they must choose how they will hold title. This is one of those decisions that feels like a formality — a box ticked at the conveyancer's office — but it can have enormous consequences for your estate plan, your tax position, and what happens if one co-owner dies, divorces, or wants to exit. Getting it wrong is expensive and complicated to unwind.

There are two main structures in Australia: joint tenancy and tenancy in common. They sound similar but operate very differently. Understanding the distinction before you buy — not after — is essential.

The Two Structures: How They Work

Joint tenancy is the default structure for married couples and de facto partners in most states. Under joint tenancy, each owner holds an equal, undivided share of the entire property — not a distinct portion of it. The defining feature of joint tenancy is the right of survivorship: if one joint tenant dies, their interest automatically passes to the surviving joint tenant(s), regardless of what the deceased's will says. The property does not form part of the deceased's estate.

Tenancy in common is different in every important way. Each co-owner holds a distinct, divisible share of the property. That share can be equal (50/50) or unequal (70/30, 99/1, or any other split). Crucially, each co-owner's share forms part of their estate on death and passes according to their will — not automatically to the other co-owner. There is no right of survivorship.

Joint Tenancy — Best For

  • Married couples seeking simple, automatic succession
  • Situations where estate complexity should be minimised
  • Equal financial contributions from both parties
  • Long-term relationships with aligned financial goals
  • When avoiding probate on the property is a priority

Tenancy in Common — Best For

  • Property investors purchasing with friends or siblings
  • Unequal financial contributions (one party contributed more)
  • Blended families needing to protect children from prior relationships
  • Sophisticated estate planning (directing share to specific beneficiaries)
  • Tax planning: allocating income to the lower-income earner

Tax Implications of Each Structure

The ownership structure you choose has direct tax consequences across CGT, income tax, and stamp duty.

Income tax: Under joint tenancy, rental income and deductions must be split 50/50 — even if one partner earns more and would benefit more from a larger deduction allocation. Under tenancy in common, income and deductions flow proportionally to the ownership shares. A higher-income earner holding 70% of the property in a tenancy in common structure receives 70% of the rental deductions, potentially saving thousands annually in income tax.

CGT on death — joint tenancy: When a joint tenant dies, their share automatically vests in the surviving co-owner. Provided the property has always been the couple's main residence, this transfer is generally free of CGT and stamp duty (concessions vary by state). The surviving owner simply becomes the sole owner. This is one of the key advantages of joint tenancy for couples — it is administratively clean and tax-efficient for simple situations.

CGT on death — tenancy in common: Under tenancy in common, the deceased's share passes according to their will. If it passes to a non-spouse beneficiary (such as children from a prior relationship), CGT and potentially stamp duty can be triggered. Careful estate planning — including testamentary trusts and specific bequests — is needed to manage this risk.

"Tenancy in common gives investors flexibility. Joint tenancy gives couples simplicity. Neither structure is universally better — the right choice depends entirely on your relationship, your estate plan, and your investment strategy."

Co-Ownership Between Friends and Siblings

With housing affordability making solo purchases increasingly difficult, more Australians are co-purchasing with friends, siblings, or extended family members. This is increasingly viable as a strategy — but it comes with significant risks that are often ignored in the enthusiasm of getting into the market.

The key risks are: diverging life plans (one party wants to sell, the other does not), relationship breakdowns between the co-owners, differing financial circumstances over time (one party cannot service their share of the mortgage), and disputes over maintenance, renovations, or tenants.

Co-Ownership Agreement: Any co-ownership between non-spouses should be governed by a formal co-ownership agreement (also called a property co-owners agreement). This legally binding document outlines each party's rights and obligations, the process for one party buying out the other, how disputes are resolved, what happens if one party cannot meet their financial obligations, and the mechanism for sale if agreement cannot be reached. This agreement is distinct from the title structure and is essential protection for everyone involved.

Changing from One Structure to Another

It is possible to change ownership structure after purchase — but it is not free or simple. Converting a joint tenancy to a tenancy in common is called "severance." In most states, one co-owner can unilaterally sever the joint tenancy by registering a notice with the titles office (without requiring the other co-owner's consent). Once severed, the ownership becomes an equal tenancy in common.

Converting from tenancy in common to joint tenancy, or changing the proportional shares in a tenancy in common, typically requires a formal transfer of the relevant interest — which may trigger stamp duty in many states, even between family members. The rules vary significantly by state, and some states have concessions for spouses. Always obtain advice before attempting to change structure after purchase, as the stamp duty implications can be substantial.

Case Studies: Making the Right Choice

Case 1 — Married couple buying their home: Sarah and James are married and buying a $950,000 home together as their primary residence. They contribute roughly equally to the deposit. Both earn similar incomes. For them, joint tenancy is the natural choice. The right of survivorship means that if either dies, the surviving spouse automatically becomes the sole owner — no probate, no delay, no complexity. Their estate plan for the rest of their assets handles the rest.

Case 2 — Siblings buying an investment property: Emma and Mark are siblings co-purchasing a $650,000 investment property. Emma contributes $180,000 (roughly 65%) to the deposit and will have a higher income going forward. Mark contributes $96,000 (35%). For them, tenancy in common at 65/35 is clearly superior. The split reflects actual contributions. Emma receives 65% of rental deductions against her higher income. Each can direct their share via their own will. And they sign a co-ownership agreement setting out exit procedures and dispute resolution before exchange.

What RICO Recommends

At RICO, our first piece of advice to any co-buying clients is: speak to your solicitor and your accountant before deciding on ownership structure. The decision interacts with your estate plan, your tax position, your relationship with the co-owner, and your long-term investment goals in ways that cannot be generalised.

Our second piece of advice: do not leave the decision to the conveyancer's standard form. Be intentional. If you are buying with a non-spouse, get a co-ownership agreement drafted. If you are buying an investment property, get advice on whether the proportional split should reflect contributions, income levels, or both. The cost of getting this right upfront is trivial compared to the cost of unwinding the wrong structure later.
